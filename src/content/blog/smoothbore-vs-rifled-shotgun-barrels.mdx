Every deer season, somebody on a forum asks if they can just drop a sabot slug into their old waterfowl pump gun and call it good. The honest answer is: you can pull the trigger, but you won't like what happens downrange. Whether you need a **smoothbore or rifled barrel** comes down to one thing — what kind of slug you're planning to shoot — and getting that wrong is the difference between a 100-yard gun and a 75-yard gun that just happens to look the same.

This isn't a knock on either setup. Plenty of hunters are perfectly equipped for their ground with a smoothbore they already own. The point is knowing which one you actually have, what it's realistically good for, and what the real ballistics say — before the season forces the question.

## What "Smoothbore" and "Rifled" Actually Mean

A **smoothbore barrel** has no internal spiral grooves — it's the same bore your turkey and waterfowl loads run through. A **rifled barrel** has those grooves cut into the bore, exactly like a rifle, and imparts spin on whatever passes through it.

That spin is the whole story. It's why the two barrel types aren't interchangeable with the same ammo if you want the gun to actually shoot straight.

## Foster Slugs vs. Sabot Slugs — Why the Barrel Has to Match

**Foster slugs** (also called rifled slugs, confusingly) are solid lead or lead-alloy projectiles with shallow rifling grooves cast into the *slug itself*, not the barrel. Those grooves don't spin the slug in flight — they let it swage down slightly to clear tighter barrel constrictions. A Foster slug flies stabilized mainly by its front-heavy, hollow-base design, the same way a badminton birdie's weight-forward shape keeps it flying nose-first. It doesn't need a rifled barrel, and running one through a rifled barrel actually hurts accuracy — the spin fights the slug's own aerodynamic stabilization and can open up your groups instead of tightening them.

**Sabot slugs** are the opposite setup: a smaller-diameter projectile (often a real rifle bullet) riding inside a plastic sabot sleeve that fills the bore. The sabot needs a rifled barrel to grab it and impart spin — that's what stabilizes the payload once the sabot peels away in flight. Run a sabot slug through a smoothbore and you get none of that stabilization. The slug tumbles — a phenomenon slug shooters call keyholing — and accuracy falls apart fast.

The rule is simple even if the naming is confusing: **Foster slugs want a smoothbore. Sabot slugs want a rifled barrel.** Match them backwards and you're not shooting a worse gun — you're shooting a gun that isn't doing its job.

A few things jump out. Foster loads top out around 2,750 ft-lbs at the muzzle and lose velocity fast — the Federal Deep Penetrator drops from 1,350 fps to 949 fps by 100 yards, a 30% loss. Sabot loads start faster (up to 2,000 fps for the Hornady SST) and hold onto energy noticeably better downrange, which is the entire reason they're rated for 150–200 yard shots instead of 75–100.

## Can You Just Add a Rifled Barrel to Your Existing Shotgun?

Often, yes. Most pump and semi-auto shotguns from major manufacturers (Mossberg, Remington, Benelli, Winchester) offer drop-in rifled slug barrels as an aftermarket part, and swapping barrels typically takes a few minutes with no gunsmithing required. Pricing runs roughly **$100–$500** depending on make, model, and barrel length, whether you buy new from the manufacturer or a retailer, or used through a marketplace like **GunBroker.com** — used barrels in good condition can be a real way to close that gap without paying full retail.

Before buying one, check two things: that the barrel is rated for sabot slugs specifically (some rifled barrels are choke-tube-compatible smoothbore barrels marketed loosely as "rifled" — read the actual spec, not just the label), and that your receiver and stock setup can mount a scope, since a rifled barrel's extended range is wasted on open rifle sights.

## Is It Worth Upgrading?

If you're in one of the SWC states — Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Iowa — you've also got a straight-wall rifle option on the table now, which changes this math. A rifled shotgun barrel closes some of the range gap with a smoothbore, but the numbers above show it still doesn't fully catch up to what a .350 Legend or .400 Legend does at 150 yards with a lot less recoil doing it. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I shoot sabot slugs out of a smoothbore barrel?

You can chamber and fire them, but don't expect accuracy. Without rifling to spin the sabot payload, the slug tumbles in flight (keyholing) and groups open up dramatically past even modest distances. If you're shooting sabots, use a rifled barrel.

### Can I shoot Foster (rifled) slugs out of a rifled barrel?

Yes, but it's not doing you any favors. Foster slugs stabilize through their own hollow-base, front-heavy design, not through spin. Adding barrel-imparted spin on top of that can actually degrade accuracy compared to running the same slug through a smoothbore.

### How do I know if my shotgun barrel is smoothbore or rifled?

Look down the bore from the breech end with the action open. Rifled barrels show visible spiral grooves running the length of the bore. A smoothbore barrel looks like a plain, uninterrupted tube. If you're not sure, a gunsmith or sporting goods counter can confirm in under a minute.

### Is a rifled slug barrel a worthwhile upgrade for an existing shotgun?

If your shots regularly stretch past 75–100 yards, yes — the ballistics show a real, meaningful gain in retained velocity and energy with a sabot setup. If you're hunting tight cover or funnels inside that range already, a rifled barrel is solving a problem you don't have.
