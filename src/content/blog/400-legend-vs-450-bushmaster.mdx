The .350 vs. .400 Legend debate is a fairly easy one — more energy for a little more recoil, take it or leave it. The .400 Legend vs. .450 Bushmaster debate is a different animal entirely. Both of these cartridges hit hard enough to anchor a Midwest whitetail. The real question isn't "which one works" — it's how much recoil you're willing to eat for how much more knockdown power, and which cartridge's real-world logistics actually fit the way you hunt.

## The Case for the .400 Legend: The Shoulder-Saver

Winchester built the .400 Legend to sit right between the .350 Legend's mild manners and the .450 Bushmaster's raw power — and on paper, it splits that difference almost perfectly.

### Why It Wins:
- **Meaningfully More Power Than the .350, Without the Bushmaster's Punch:** At around 17 ft-lbs of felt recoil in a typical 7-lb rifle, the .400 Legend shoots like a stout .308 Winchester — a real step up from the .350 Legend, but nowhere near the shoulder-thump of the Bushmaster.
- **A Flatter, More Forgiving Trajectory:** The .400 Legend holds onto more energy and drops less at 200+ yards than its case dimensions might suggest — useful if you hunt field edges or power lines where a longer shot is possible.
- **Simple Bolt-Action Logistics:** In a bolt gun, the .400 Legend is just as straightforward to own and shoot as any other short-action cartridge. The AR-15 conversion story is more involved (more on that below), but if you're buying a dedicated bolt rifle, there's nothing exotic here.

**Best For:** Hunters who want a genuine upgrade over the .350 Legend's stopping power but aren't willing to trade away shootability to get it, and anyone who wants a flatter-shooting option for field-edge or power-line shots out to 200 yards.

## The Case for the .450 Bushmaster: The Sledgehammer

The .450 Bushmaster earned its reputation the old-fashioned way — with a big, slow, .452-inch bullet that hits like a freight train at typical Midwest whitetail ranges.

### Why It Wins:
- **The Hardest-Hitting Straight-Wall Option:** A .452-inch bullet moving at Bushmaster velocities creates a wound channel that few other straight-wall cartridges can match, especially inside 150 yards.
- **The Deepest Ammo Bench:** Federal, Hornady, Remington, and Winchester all load .450 Bushmaster, and a wide range of aftermarket parts and magazines exist simply because the cartridge has been around since 2007. That maturity shows up as real convenience at the counter.
- **A Track Record in Thick Cover:** For hunters working tight timber or brushy river bottoms where shots rarely stretch past 100–125 yards, raw stopping power matters more than a flat trajectory — and this is where the Bushmaster is most at home.

**Best For:** Hunters working thick cover or close-range stands who prioritize raw stopping power over shootability, and anyone building an AR-pattern rifle who wants to tap into an already mature ecosystem of parts and ammunition.

## Recoil & Shootability: Where the Real Difference Lives

If ballistics are this close, why does anyone choose the Bushmaster over the .400 Legend? Mostly tradition, ammo availability, and bullet weight options — because on recoil, the .400 Legend is the clear winner.

- The **.400 Legend** generates roughly 17 ft-lbs of free recoil energy in a typical 7-lb rifle — noticeably stouter than the .350 Legend, but still very manageable, similar to a .308 Winchester.
- The **.450 Bushmaster** runs meaningfully higher: figures generally fall between 20 and 27 ft-lbs depending on rifle weight and load, with lighter AR-pattern rifles landing at the high end of that range. That's a real step up — enough that flinching becomes a genuine risk for less experienced shooters.

> **Ethical Note:** A cartridge with more paper energy doesn't help you if the recoil makes you anticipate the shot. If you're choosing between these two and you're not already comfortable with heavy-recoiling rifles, the .400 Legend is very likely to produce better real-world shot placement — which matters more than the ~100–150 ft-lbs of extra retained energy the Bushmaster carries at 200 yards.

## Ammo & Platform Realities: What the Gun Shops Won't Tell You

### Ammo Availability

This is the .450 Bushmaster's biggest advantage. It's been on the market since 2007 and is loaded by Federal, Hornady, Remington, and Winchester, among others — you'll find it in most well-stocked sporting goods stores and plenty of small-town shops. The .400 Legend, released in 2023, remains a Winchester-exclusive cartridge for now, so if you're counting on finding a spare box on opening weekend in a rural town, the Bushmaster gives you better odds.

### The AR-15 Conversion Reality

Neither of these cartridges drops into a stock 5.56 AR-15 with just a barrel swap — both require a bolt with an opened-up bolt face, along with a dedicated upper.

- **.450 Bushmaster:** Needs a new bolt and upper. It often uses the same physical magazine footprint as a standard AR-15 magazine body, but requires single-stack followers and altered internal feed ribs to prevent the wide .500" case from bulging against double-stack walls — dedicated magazines (Duramag, ASC, and similar) handle this properly, where generic AR-15 mags often won't.
- **.400 Legend:** Needs a .422" bolt face — the same one used for 6.8 SPC and .224 Valkyrie builds, which actually makes finding a compatible bolt easy even though the cartridge itself is new. Magazines are the harder part: the .400 Legend's case body is wider than 6.8 SPC's (closer to 6.5 Grendel dimensions), so off-the-shelf 6.8 SPC magazines aren't a reliable drop-in. Purpose-built .400 Legend magazines (Duramag and others) are the dependable route.

Net result: the Bushmaster's decade-plus head start means more aftermarket parts, more magazine options, and more shops that can order what you need without a special trip. ## The Verdict: Sledgehammer or Shoulder-Saver?

Neither cartridge is "wrong" here — it comes down to how much recoil you're willing to trade for how much extra stopping power and market maturity you want.

### Choose the .400 Legend if:
- You want a meaningful step up from the .350 Legend without entering heavy-recoil territory.
- You hunt field edges, food plots, or power lines where a 200-yard shot is a real possibility.
- You don't mind a narrower, currently Winchester-only ammo selection in exchange for noticeably softer recoil.

### Choose the .450 Bushmaster if:
- You hunt thick cover or close-range stands where raw stopping power matters more than trajectory.
- You want the widest possible ammo and aftermarket support, especially for an AR-pattern build.
- You're an experienced shooter who isn't bothered by a real step up in recoil.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is .450 Bushmaster harder-hitting than .400 Legend?** At the muzzle, yes — the Bushmaster's heavier .452-inch bullet carries more raw energy. But once you match zeros at 150 yards, the two are remarkably close out to 200 yards, with the .400 Legend actually pulling slightly ahead in retained energy and trajectory beyond that range.

**Which has less recoil?** The .400 Legend, clearly. At roughly 17 ft-lbs of felt recoil versus the Bushmaster's 20–27 ft-lbs, it's a noticeably softer-shooting round in a comparable rifle weight.

**Which is easier to find ammo for?** The .450 Bushmaster. It's been on the market since 2007 and is loaded by four major manufacturers, while the .400 Legend remains Winchester-exclusive for now.
