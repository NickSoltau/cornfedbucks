## 2. The "Why": A Brief History of Straight-Wall Regulations

To understand the sudden explosion of these cartridges, you have to look at the unique geography of Midwestern hunting.

States like Ohio, Michigan, Iowa, and Indiana are characterized by a mix of dense woodlots, sprawling agricultural fields, and relatively high population densities. Historically, wildlife managers banned traditional, high-velocity bottleneck rifle cartridges — like the venerable .30-06 Springfield or .270 Winchester — out of safety concerns. A sleek, aerodynamic bottleneck bullet fired at a shallow angle can travel for miles across flat farmland if a hunter misses a target. Shotgun slugs and heavy round-ball muzzleloaders, by comparison, lose altitude and energy quickly, limiting their maximum travel distance.

However, shotguns are inherently limited in efficiency and precision. Enter the compromise: the straight-wall cartridge rule.

By allowing centerfire rifle cartridges with no neck taper (the case walls must run straight from the base to the mouth), states found a perfect middle ground. Modern straight-wall rounds deliver incredible terminal energy and pinpoint accuracy out to 150–200 yards, but their blunt, heavy bullet designs cause them to drop like stones shortly after. You get the surgical precision of a modern rifle platform within ethical hunting distances, while the state maintains the safety margins required for populated rural areas.

While the exact caliber window and case-length rules differ from state to state — and, in Michigan's case, by region within the state — the shift has been an overwhelming success for hunter retention and ethical herd management.

> **Two Exceptions Worth Knowing:** Indiana expanded its rifle rules in 2025 and no longer requires a straight-wall cartridge at all — hunters there may now use any centerfire rifle with a bullet diameter of 5.56mm (.223") or larger. Michigan is following the same path: the Natural Resources Commission's Amendment No. 6 of 2026, approved in May 2026, eliminates the historic Lower Peninsula "rifle line" starting this fall, opening the Limited Firearm Zone to traditional bottleneck rifles alongside straight-wall cartridges. Ohio and Iowa remain straight-wall-specific.
>
> Straight-wall cartridges remain fully legal in both Indiana and Michigan — they're just no longer the only option. We're keeping both states in our coverage rather than writing them off, because plenty of hunters in Indiana and Michigan already bought a straight-wall rifle over the last decade and aren't going to replace a rifle they like just because a bottleneck option became legal too. If that's you, this guide stays exactly as relevant as it was before these rule changes.

## 3. The Caliber Breakdown: Heavy Hitters & Fast Movers

The straight-wall market has exploded, resulting in a handful of distinct cartridges competing for space on the retail shelves. Let's look at the core contenders dominating the corn and bean fields today.

### .350 Legend

Introduced by Winchester, the .350 Legend is the undisputed king of efficiency. Designed specifically to maximize compliance rules while fitting cleanly into a standard AR-15 magazine or a lightweight bolt-action rifle, it is incredibly soft-shooting. Delivering recoil that feels remarkably similar to a .243 Winchester, it is the perfect choice for youth, smaller-framed hunters, or anyone tired of getting flinched by heavy recoil. Don't let the mild manners fool you; it offers a flat trajectory out to 150 yards and excellent terminal performance on whitetails. There has been some debate as to the effectiveness of the .350 Legend, with the top complaint being that it leaves little to no bloodtrail if tracking your deer after the shot is needed. Some of this can be mitigated by your ammunition selection, but many hunters choose to solve the issue by switching to the .400 Legend.

### .400 Legend

The newer sibling to the .350, the .400 Legend was engineered for hunters who want a bit more "thump" without sacrificing the shootability of a modern platform. Launching a wider, heavier bullet, it offers roughly 20% more energy than the .350 Legend while mimicking its flat trajectory. It is rapidly becoming the goldilocks choice for hunters who want a definitive middle ground between light recoil and heavy bullet mass. While the .400 Legend is catching on quickly, hunters have mentioned finding ammunition can be harder than some of the others mentioned here.

### .450 Bushmaster

If you want raw, unadulterated knockdown power, the .450 Bushmaster is the original heavyweight champion. Firing a massive .452-caliber bullet, this round hits like a freight train and leaves devastating blood trails. It is an incredibly effective cartridge, but it comes at a cost: substantial recoil and louder muzzle blast. If you don't mind a rifle that pushes back, the Bushmaster is a proven stopper. If you want to tame that recoil, an adhesive or screw-on pad like this one from <a href="https://amzn.to/44iu7t9" rel="sponsored noopener" target="_blank">Tourbon</a> works well, or you can use a slip-on pad more commonly seen on shotguns, like this one from <a href="https://amzn.to/4vZE3DS" rel="sponsored noopener" target="_blank">Allen</a>.

### .360 Buckhammer

Developed by Remington to revitalize the traditional lever-action rifle, the .360 Buckhammer utilizes a rimmed case optimized for slick-cycling lever guns. Ballistically, it pushes a .358-caliber bullet at impressive velocities, offering high energy transfer and a slightly flatter trajectory than traditional lever-action rounds like the old .30-30 within 150 yards. Like the .400 Legend, sourcing ammo can be harder, but it's a lever action, and lever actions are cool.

### .45-70 Government

The grandfather of the group. While designed in the 1870s, this classic black-powder-era cartridge found a massive second life thanks to modern straight-wall rules that grandfathered it in. Fired out of robust lever-actions, modern smokeless loads turn the .45-70 into a devastating short-range powerhouse. It is heavy, it kicks hard, but it has over a century of proven big-game history behind it. Some models can make mounting a scope difficult. If you are hunting in an area with thick brush, this might be better suited than some of the lighter rifles like the .350 Legend. For the kick, you can add a recoil pad like the ones mentioned for the .450 Bushmaster above.

## 4. Ammunition & Magazine Capacity: What You Can Actually Carry Afield

Caliber isn't the only rule governing your rifle in the field. Every straight-wall state also caps how much ammunition you can have loaded — in the chamber, the magazine, or both — while hunting. Get this wrong and it's a citation regardless of how compliant your cartridge choice is.

- **Ohio:** Shotguns and straight-wall cartridge rifles are limited to three rounds total, chamber and magazine combined — the same three-shell rule that applies statewide for gun season.
- **Michigan:** Semi-automatic rifles and shotguns are capped at six rounds total (five in the magazine, one in the chamber) statewide, in both the Northern Zone and the Limited Firearm Zone. Bolt-action and lever-action rifles aren't subject to this magazine cap. Handguns used in the Limited Firearm Zone carry a separate nine-round combined limit.
- **Indiana:** Hunters may not possess more than ten rifle cartridges while afield hunting deer — a possession limit that applies regardless of magazine size.
- **Iowa:** There is no maximum magazine capacity for rifles or shotguns used for deer hunting.

The practical upshot: if you're building a straight-wall AR-15 for Ohio or Michigan, budget for a capacity-limited magazine or a mechanical limiter alongside your barrel and optic. Bolt-action and lever-action platforms sidestep this issue almost entirely, since most factory magazines already hold well under these caps.

> **Always Double-Check:** Magazine and possession limits get revised more often than caliber rules do. Confirm the current-year numbers in your state's hunting digest before the season opens, especially if you hunt across state lines.

## 5. The Gear Guide: Rifle Platforms & Optic Systems

Picking your cartridge is only half the battle; you need to match it to the right mechanical platform and optic system to build an effective Midwest rifle.

### Choosing Your Action

- **Bolt-Action Rifles:** The choice for pure efficiency. Bolt guns are inherently accurate, lightweight to carry through the timber or across the field, and typically the most budget-friendly option on the market. They are simple to maintain and excel when you have time for a steady, calculated single shot.
- **Lever-Action Rifles:** For the traditionalist. There is nothing quite like the balance of a classic lever gun in a deer stand. They offer incredibly fast follow-up shots and carry beautifully in the hand, making them the ultimate brush guns for cartridges like the .360 Buckhammer and .45-70.
- **AR-15 / Modern Sporting Rifles (MSR):** The modular approach. Building or buying a straight-wall AR upper gives you unparalleled ergonomics, adjustable stocks for changing winter layers, and excellent inherent recoil mitigation because the gas system absorbs a portion of the backward force. It is worth noting here that some AR platforms chambered in straight-wall cartridges have reported feeding issues with certain aftermarket magazines. Stick with a magazine designed specifically for your cartridge, and test it thoroughly before the season opens.

### Optics: Beating the Rainbow Trajectory

Because straight-wall bullets are heavy and blunt, they do not fly like laser beams. They possess a noticeable "rainbow" trajectory. A traditional duplex scope sighted in dead-on at 100 yards will leave you guessing blindly if a trophy buck steps out at 180 yards.

## 6. Ballistics & Shooting Realities: Respecting the "200-Yard Wall"

The single most critical concept a straight-wall hunter must master is the "200-yard wall." Unlike sleek, aerodynamic bottleneck cartridges built to split the wind over long distances, straight-wall projectiles are short, fat, and heavy. They lose velocity rapidly the second they exit the muzzle.

To illustrate this drop-off, look at the typical real-world trajectory of a standard 150-grain .350 Legend or 250-grain .450 Bushmaster sighted in dead-on at 100 yards:

- **50 Yards:** +1.5 inches
- **100 Yards:** 0 inches (Dead On)
- **150 Yards:** -3.5 to -4.5 inches
- **200 Yards:** -10 to -13 inches
- **250 Yards:** -24 to -30 inches (The Cliff)

Past 200 yards, the bullet hits a ballistic cliff. A minor distance miscalculation of just 15 yards can mean the difference between a clean heart-and-lung shot and a clean miss — or worse, a wounded animal. Furthermore, because these heavy bullets travel at slower velocities, crosswinds push them aggressively. A 10-mph crosswind that barely moves a deer hunter's bottleneck rifle round can drift a straight-wall bullet 5 to 7 inches off-target at 175 yards.

Ethical marksmanship dictates knowing your personal limits. While straight-wall platforms are phenomenally precise out to 150 yards, executing a shot past 175 yards requires an exact range reading, an understanding of the wind, and a rock-solid steady rest. Practice diligently from field positions, not just off sandbags, to guarantee success when the moment of truth arrives.

## 7. The Shotgun-to-Rifle Crossover Note

If you are still hitting the woods every autumn with a rifled 12-gauge or 20-gauge slug gun, you are likely tolerating compromises that you don't have to anymore. Slug guns are notorious for punishing recoil, expensive ammunition that often costs upwards of five dollars a shell, and accuracy that can be erratic depending on the barrel temperature and sabot choice.

Transitioning to a modern straight-wall platform like the .350 or .400 Legend will completely change your experience in the woods. You cut your recoil profile by more than half, shrink your average groups to easily under two inches at 100 yards, and significantly lower your cost per shot at the retail counter. It makes pre-season practice enjoyable rather than a chore, building the confidence necessary to make tough shots count when it matters most.
