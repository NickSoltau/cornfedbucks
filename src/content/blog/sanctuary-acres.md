Every land management article starts with seed blends. None of them start with the part that actually moves the needle on small Midwest parcels: a sanctuary acre you never walk into.

## The math on leased ground

Most guys hunting leased row-crop ground are working with 40 to 160 acres, surrounded by other hunters doing the same thing. You are not going to out-food-plot your neighbors — they've got the same co-op, the same seed catalog, and probably more tillable acres than you. What you can do that they likely aren't doing is give deer somewhere to feel safe.

A one- to three-acre block of thick cover that gets zero foot traffic, year-round, does more for daylight buck movement on small ground than a half-acre plot of clover ever will. The deer using your parcel already know where the food is — it's everywhere, it's corn and beans. What they don't have is somewhere to bed that doesn't have a stand fifty yards away.

## What makes a sanctuary actually work

- No entry, ever — not to check cameras, not to "just peek," not in the off-season
- Located off the property's most obvious access route, not centrally
- Thick at deer-eye level — a mature timber stand with no understory doesn't count

If you only do one land management project this year and you're working with limited acreage, skip the plot and fence off the sanctuary instead. You can always add a plot next year. You can't un-pressure a bedding area once deer have decided it isn't safe.
