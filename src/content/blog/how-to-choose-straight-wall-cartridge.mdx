Spend five minutes on any hunting forum and you'll see the same question repeated endlessly: *"Which straight-wall cartridge is the best?"*

Here's the truth: there isn't one. There's only the cartridge that matches how, where, and what you hunt. Strip away the marketing, and choosing the right SWC comes down to exactly three variables — **your recoil tolerance, your preferred rifle platform, and your typical shot distance.** Everything else is secondary.

Here's how to work through those three to land on your cartridge.

## 1. Start With Your Recoil Tolerance

Before you look at ballistics or downrange energy, be honest about recoil. A cartridge that makes you flinch is inherently less accurate than one you can shoot comfortably, no matter how hard it hits on paper.

Think of recoil tolerance in three tiers:

- **Low tolerance** (youth, new hunters, or precision-focused shooters): you want a rifle you can practice with all summer without dreading it.
- **Moderate tolerance** (experienced deer hunters used to a .243 Win or .30-30 Win): a solid, noticeable push doesn't bother you.
- **High tolerance** (experienced big-game hunters): you'll take heavy, traditional slug-gun-style recoil if it means maximum stopping power.

Here's how the field actually stacks up in free recoil energy, in a standard-weight rifle:

- **.350 Legend:** ~8 ft-lbs — mild, similar to a .243 Win
- **.400 Legend:** ~17 ft-lbs — moderate, similar to a .30-30 Win
- **.360 Buckhammer:** ~17–19 ft-lbs — close to .400 Legend, slightly ahead of .350 Legend
- **.450 Bushmaster:** ~20–27 ft-lbs — heavy, comparable to a light 12-gauge slug
- **.45-70 Government:** ~25–35 ft-lbs — the top of the range, on par with or beyond a full 12-gauge slug depending on rifle weight and load

*(For a deeper dive into felt recoil and how rifle weight changes these numbers, see Field Note 005 and Field Note 006.)*

## 2. Consider Your Rifle Platform

Your cartridge choice decides what kind of rifle you're carrying. Straight-wall cartridges split into two families: modern rimless/rebated designs built for bolt-actions and AR-15s, and traditional rimmed designs built for lever-actions.

### Bolt-Action or AR-15: The Modern Three

If you want a bolt-action or a modular AR-15, your options are .350 Legend, .400 Legend, and .450 Bushmaster — and each has a clear reason to pick it.

- **.350 Legend wins on simplicity.** It uses a standard 5.56/.223 bolt face, so converting an AR-15 is just an upper swap and dedicated magazines — nothing exotic.
- **.400 Legend splits the difference.** It also runs on a .223-sized bolt face and standard-length AR-15 magazines, giving you meaningfully more energy than .350 Legend without stepping up to a full-recoil round.
- **.450 Bushmaster hits hardest.** It needs a larger bolt face than the Legend family (most AR-15 .450 Bushmaster uppers pair with a .223-pattern lower using a modified magazine, or a dedicated large-bore bolt carrier group), so it's a bigger conversion — but it delivers the most raw energy of the bolt/AR options.

### Lever-Action: The Rimmed Classics

If you'd rather run a lever-gun — a growing trend among Midwest hunters who like the classic feel — your two main options are .360 Buckhammer and .45-70 Government.

- **.360 Buckhammer** was purpose-built for this: a rimmed cartridge designed to headspace and feed reliably in lever-actions, with genuinely flat-shooting, modern ballistics for the platform. It's worth knowing that .360 Buckhammer isn't just a rimmed clone of .350 Legend — it runs roughly 300 fps faster with better energy retention downrange, which matters if you're deciding between the two purely on ballistics.
- **.45-70 Government** is the old warhorse — over 150 years old, still hugely popular, and available in everything from budget Henry lever guns to premium Marlins. It hits harder than any Legend-family round but drops faster past 150 yards and kicks noticeably more.

## 3. Match It to Your Typical Shot Distance

The last variable is where you actually take your shots. Straight-wall cartridges are, by design, a "close-to-medium range" category — none of them shoot as flat as a .270 Win or 6.5 Creedmoor — but there's still a meaningful spread inside that category.

- **Inside 150 yards:** every cartridge on this list performs well here. This is where recoil tolerance and platform preference should drive the decision more than ballistics.
- **Up to 175–200 yards:** this is where .400 Legend and .360 Buckhammer start to separate from .350 Legend, carrying more energy and a flatter trajectory to the edge of straight-wall range.
- **Inside 100–125 yards, especially in timber:** this is .45-70 territory. Its trajectory drops off faster past that window, but inside it, few rounds hit harder.

## Two Exceptions

> **Two Exceptions:** if you hunt in Indiana, none of this changes your legal options — Indiana removed its straight-wall requirement in 2025 and now allows most centerfire rifles. Michigan is following the same path this fall: the Natural Resources Commission's Amendment No. 6 of 2026 eliminates the historic Lower Peninsula "rifle line," opening the old Limited Firearm Zone to traditional bottleneck rifles too. This framework still applies if you're choosing a dedicated straight-wall rifle by preference — and plenty of hunters in both states will, since a rifle you already own and trust doesn't stop being a good choice just because a bottleneck option became legal alongside it. You're just no longer legally boxed into this category the way Ohio and Iowa hunters are. ## The Bottom Line

There's no universal "best" straight-wall cartridge — only the one that fits your recoil tolerance, your platform, and your typical shot distance. Work through those three questions honestly, and the right cartridge for you becomes obvious.
