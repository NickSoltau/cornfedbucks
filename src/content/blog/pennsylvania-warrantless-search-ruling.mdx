On July 21, 2026, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court ruled 7-0 that **game wardens can no longer walk onto clearly posted, gated private land without a warrant**. If you hunt leased or owned ground in a state that still allows warrantless entry, this is worth understanding — not because it changes your state's law today, but because it's part of a pattern that's spreading state by state.

*H/t to [Stephen Ziegler of Rack Junkies](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC9w9ao6bHcpkRUGyqiWg87w), whose coverage flagged this ruling — his podcast digs into a lot of hunting-policy stories like this one worth following.*

## The Case: Two Hunting Clubs, 5,500 Acres, One Trail Camera

Two hunting clubs in Clearfield County — Punxsutawney (4,400 acres) and Pitch Pine (1,100 acres), roughly 5,500 acres between them — sued the Pennsylvania Game Commission and Warden Mark Gritzer. Both properties were fully gated, locked, and posted.

According to the court's opinion, Gritzer and other officers entered the clubs' land without consent, a warrant, or probable cause **at least 15 to 22 times since 2013**. At one point, Gritzer placed a trail camera on Punxsutawney's property for **78 days** trying to build a case for illegal elk feeding.

The clubs argued this violated Article I, Section 8 of the Pennsylvania Constitution, which protects "possessions" — a term the court found historically included land — from unreasonable searches. The ruling overturns *Commonwealth v. Russo* (2007), which had said the opposite.

The state's attorney argued the court would be discarding "over 100 years of precedent" tied to the federal open fields doctrine — a 1924 U.S. Supreme Court rule that strips Fourth Amendment protection from land outside the immediate curtilage of a home. The Pennsylvania court's answer: the state constitution isn't required to follow federal precedent when its own text says something different.

## What This Doesn't Change for Corn Belt Hunters

**This ruling applies to Pennsylvania.** It does not change the law in Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, or Iowa. Game wardens in all four of those states can currently still enter posted private land without a warrant under the federal open fields doctrine, unless a specific state case says otherwise. For where each of our core states actually stands on hunting law, see our [straight-wall cartridge state regulations guide](/blog/straight-wall-cartridge-state-regulations). If you lease [sanctuary acres](/blog/sanctuary-acres) or any other ground for deer season, don't let this headline give you a false sense of where your own state stands — check locally before you assume anything's changed.

## Where Else This Is Happening

Pennsylvania is now the **eighth state** whose top court has rejected the open fields doctrine under its own constitution, joining Mississippi, Montana, New York, Oregon, Tennessee, Vermont, and Washington. The Institute for Justice — the property-rights firm that brought the Pennsylvania case — currently has similar active challenges in **Alabama, Louisiana, and Virginia**, all built on the same argument: that a state constitution protecting "possessions" from unreasonable searches covers land, not just personal belongings.

Tennessee's case is the closest parallel for hunters: in 2024, its Court of Appeals ruled the same way after wardens hung trail cameras on private land there, and the state chose not to appeal.

## The Bottom Line

If you lease or own ground for deer season, this case is a reminder that "posted" isn't just a courtesy sign in every state — in a growing number of them, it's a legal line the government now has to respect too. Whether that becomes true where you hunt depends on your own state constitution's language and whether anyone's willing to challenge it in court. Worth watching, not worth assuming.

## FAQ

**Does this ruling apply outside Pennsylvania?**
No. It's binding only in Pennsylvania. Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, and Iowa still operate under the federal open fields doctrine unless their own courts rule otherwise.

**What is the "open fields doctrine"?**
A 1924 U.S. Supreme Court rule holding that the Fourth Amendment doesn't protect private land outside the small area immediately around a home, even if it's fenced and posted.

**Can game wardens still enter posted land in Pennsylvania at all?**
Yes, in limited situations — during emergencies, when they can see a violation from public property, or with a warrant based on probable cause. What they can no longer do is enter freely just because wildlife might be present.

**Are other states likely to follow?**
Possibly. Active challenges are underway in Alabama, Louisiana, and Virginia, using the same state-constitution argument that succeeded in Pennsylvania and Tennessee.
