If you're shopping for the best .400 Legend ammo for deer hunting this season, here's something worth genuinely getting excited about: **the .400 Legend ammo market just went from a monopoly to a real, crowded field in under a year.** Winchester introduced the cartridge in 2023 and was the only company loading it for two and a half years. Then, starting in January 2026, the floodgates opened — Nosler launched its Whitetail Country load at SHOT Show, Hornady followed with American Whitetail in March, and both Remington's Core-Lokt and Federal's Power-Shok have joined since. As of this writing, that's **five manufacturers and seven total loads** — up from one manufacturer and three loads just six months ago.

For hunters heading into this season, that's genuinely good news. You're no longer stuck with whatever Winchester happened to load — you've got real choice in bullet weight, bullet design, and price point, and every major American ammunition manufacturer has now put its own engineering behind this cartridge. Whichever load you pick, you can walk into the woods this fall knowing you did the homework on a lineup that's actually worth comparing. ## Why the .400 Legend Ammo Lineup Grew So Fast

This kind of rapid-fire manufacturer buy-in is a strong signal in itself. Ammunition companies don't commit tooling and shelf space to a cartridge that isn't moving — Winchester's own marketing has pointed to the .400 Legend's growing popularity as a straight-wall option that splits the difference between the milder .350 Legend and the harder-hitting .450 Bushmaster, and the rest of the industry has clearly taken notice. For hunters in Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Iowa, and other straight-wall states, that competition is already showing up as more choices on store shelves and, in some cases, better pricing than you'd expect for a cartridge this young.

**A regulatory note worth knowing:** two of the traditional straight-wall states are actively moving away from a straight-wall-only requirement. Indiana's Senate Enrolled Act 280 (2025) opened the state to any centerfire rifle cartridge .219 caliber (5.56mm) or larger, on both public and private land — not just straight-wall options. Michigan is following a similar path: the Natural Resources Commission's Amendment No. 6 of 2026, approved in May 2026, eliminates the historic Lower Peninsula "rifle line" starting this fall, allowing traditional bottleneck rifles alongside straight-wall cartridges in zones that used to be straight-wall/shotgun/muzzleloader-only.

Straight-wall cartridges like .400 Legend remain fully legal in both states — they're simply no longer the only legal option. That's why we're keeping Indiana and Michigan in our coverage rather than writing them off: plenty of hunters in both states already bought a straight-wall rifle over the last decade and aren't going to replace a rifle they like just because a bottleneck option became legal too. If you're one of them, this content stays exactly as relevant to you as it was before the rule change. We'll keep tracking this as more states finalize their 2026 regulations.

## How to Choose Between Them

With five manufacturers now in the mix, choosing your .400 Legend load actually comes down to a few real questions rather than "whatever Winchester happens to make":

- **Do you want the flattest trajectory, or more bullet weight?** The Deer Season XP's 190-grain polymer tip and Nosler's 215-grain Straight-Wall bullet are both engineered specifically for expansion at straight-wall velocities — strong defaults for most hunters. The Power-Point, Core-Lokt, Power-Shok, and American Whitetail all cluster in the 210–215 grain traditional soft-point range if you want a heavier, more conventional bullet.
- **Do you want to spend as little as possible, or pay for a specialized bullet design?** Federal Power-Shok is the clear budget pick among the traditional soft points. The Deer Season XP and Whitetail Country command a bit more for their purpose-built expansion designs.
- **Do you already trust a specific manufacturer's bullet in other calibers?** If you've hunted for years with Core-Lokt, InterLock, or Nosler bullets in other cartridges, .400 Legend now lets you stick with that same trusted design rather than switching brands just because of caliber.

All six practical hunting loads deliver enough energy for ethical shots inside .400 Legend's effective range, which tops out around 200 yards for most hunters — consistent with the "200-yard wall" we've covered in our [pillar guide](/blog/ultimate-guide-straight-wall-cartridge-hunting) for straight-wall cartridges generally. ## Why Some Hunters Are Handloading Their Own .400 Legend Rounds

Even with five manufacturers now loading factory ammo, a real handloading community has grown up around .400 Legend faster than you'd expect for a cartridge this young — a holdover from the early days when Winchester was the only game in town. Reloading forums have active, ongoing threads specifically about it.

The short version: .400 Legend's cartridge overall length is tight, which limits which bullets will actually fit and feed reliably — especially from a magazine rather than single-shot.

Rather than waiting on dedicated .400 Legend projectiles from bullet makers, reloaders quickly realized that the .40-caliber smokeless muzzleloader world already had a mature selection of high-quality bullets in the right diameter, since smokeless muzzleloaders have used .40-caliber projectiles for years. Bullets from makers like Bear Creek Ballistics, Pittman, and existing Hornady and Barnes .40-caliber options have all shown up in handloader discussions as viable options, alongside brass and dies now sold specifically for the cartridge.

A word of caution if this route interests you: **load development is not something to improvise from forum posts.** The right approach is published data from a bullet or powder manufacturer, or a current reloading manual — not charge weights pieced together from online discussion threads, however well-intentioned. If you're new to reloading, start with your component manufacturer's own published data and, ideally, guidance from an experienced reloader who can walk you through proper load development and pressure signs in person.

For the overwhelming majority of hunters, factory ammo remains the simpler, safer, and entirely sufficient choice — especially now that there are six genuinely good options to choose from. The handloading angle here is really about understanding why the cartridge has developed the way it has, not a recommendation to skip factory ammo.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many companies currently make .400 Legend ammo?** Five, as of this writing — Winchester (since 2023, three loads), Nosler (since January 2026), Hornady (since March 2026), and both Remington and Federal, who've joined most recently. That's up from just one manufacturer as recently as late 2025.

**Why did so many companies start making .400 Legend ammo all at once?** Ammunition manufacturers commit tooling and shelf space based on demonstrated demand, and the rapid-fire launches from Nosler, Hornady, Remington, and Federal within a matter of months strongly suggest the cartridge has been selling well since its 2023 introduction. For hunters, that's a good sign the cartridge has real staying power rather than being a passing trend.

**What's the best .400 Legend load for deer hunting?** The Winchester Deer Season XP 190-grain Extreme Point is the flattest-shooting, fastest-expanding option and a strong default choice for most Midwest whitetail hunters. Nosler's Whitetail Country, Federal's Power-Shok, Remington's Core-Lokt, and Hornady's American Whitetail are all solid alternatives depending on your bullet weight and brand preference.

**Is the Winchester Super Suppressed load good for hunting?** Not as a primary choice. It's a heavy, deliberately slow subsonic load built for suppressor use, and it trades away the flat trajectory that makes the other loads effective hunting rounds. It has a specific purpose, but that purpose isn't maximizing hunting performance.

**Can I handload my own .400 Legend ammunition?** Yes — a real handloading community has developed around the cartridge, largely from the early days when factory bullet selection was limited and the cartridge's overall length constrains bullet choice. Reloaders often pull from the existing .40-caliber smokeless muzzleloader bullet lineup. If you go this route, always work from published manufacturer load data or a current reloading manual rather than forum-sourced charge weights.

**What's the effective range of .400 Legend?** Most factory loads deliver enough energy for ethical shots out to around 200 yards, consistent with the general "200-yard wall" that applies to straight-wall cartridges as a category.
