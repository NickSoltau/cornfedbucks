If you hunt anywhere near a straight-wall cartridge state, you already know the drill: check the regs every single year, because they change more than people expect. In the last two seasons alone, Indiana dropped its case-shape requirement entirely, Michigan erased its Lower Peninsula rifle line, and Tennessee added a brand-new straight-wall season from scratch. Meanwhile Ohio hasn't budged since 2014.

This guide breaks down which **straight-wall cartridge states** exist right now, what each one actually requires, and — because laws like these change every legislative session — links straight to the source so you can verify before you buy ammo or load up.

## The short version: what changed for 2026

Three states loosened their rules this year, and one added a brand-new one:

- **Indiana** no longer requires straight-wall case shape at all. Any centerfire rifle cartridge with a bullet diameter of .219" (5.56mm) or larger is legal, on public or private land.
- **Iowa** dropped its shape requirement too — rifles just need an expanding bullet .350"–.500" in diameter with 500+ ft-lbs of muzzle energy. Bottleneck cartridges that meet those numbers now qualify.
- **Michigan** eliminated its Limited Firearms Deer Zone in the Lower Peninsula for 2026, opening the door to "all legal firearms, including bottleneck cartridges," across the whole zone.
- **Tennessee** is the one going the other direction: a brand-new 7-day straight-wall season on private land only, Nov. 14–20, 2026.

Net effect: the "core four" SWC-mandate states most hunters think of (Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Iowa) are down to really just **Ohio**. The other three have moved to performance-based standards or dropped the shape requirement altogether. That doesn't mean straight-wall cartridges got worse — a .350 Legend or .400 Legend is still a great choice in all four — it just means the *law* no longer forces the issue in three of them.

## Why these rules exist in the first place

Every one of these laws traces back to the same concern: population density and terrain. States with flat, open, heavily farmed ground and a lot of people living close to hunting land didn't want traditional bottleneck rifle cartridges in play, because they carry dangerous energy well past 400–500 yards. A straight-wall cartridge sheds velocity fast enough that its practical range tops out around 200 yards for most loads — plenty for row-crop whitetail, but a lot less likely to cause a problem if a shot goes long.

That's also why you're seeing the recent split in approach. Indiana, Iowa, and Michigan moved to performance-based standards (diameter and energy) instead of policing case shape, because the actual safety goal — limiting range — can be met without dictating whether the brass tapers. Tennessee, on the other hand, is opening a brand-new door and is being conservative about it: private land, single-shot, one narrow week.

## What this means if you're picking a cartridge

If you hunt Ohio, Wisconsin, West Virginia, Maryland, Minnesota, Illinois, Pennsylvania's special zones, Arkansas's alternative season, or Tennessee's new week, the shape of your cartridge still matters — pick something that's genuinely straight-wall. If you hunt Indiana, Iowa, or Michigan's former Limited Firearms Zone, you technically have more options now, but a straight-wall cartridge like the .350 Legend or .400 Legend is still a smart, low-recoil choice for realistic row-crop shot distances. The law loosened; the ballistics didn't change.
