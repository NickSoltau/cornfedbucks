A .350 Legend rifle without the right scope is only half a setup. As we covered in our [Ultimate Straight-Wall Cartridge Guide](/blog/ultimate-guide-straight-wall-cartridge-hunting), straight-wall bullets fly on a "rainbow" trajectory — heavy, blunt, and fast to drop once they leave the barrel. A duplex reticle sighted dead-on at 100 yards will leave you guessing if a buck steps out at 175. You need glass built around a **Bullet Drop Compensating (BDC) reticle** calibrated for exactly this kind of drop.

Here are three scopes that actually do that job well, at three different price points and use cases.

## What to Look For

Before the picks, a quick framework — because "BDC" alone doesn't tell you enough:

- **A reticle actually calibrated for straight-wall ballistics.** A generic BDC built for a flat-shooting .223 or .308 won't match your .350 Legend's drop curve. Look for scopes explicitly built or dialed for straight-wall cartridges.
- **Low-light performance.** Whitetail move at dawn and dusk. A bigger objective lens or an illuminated reticle both help you see — and aim — in the last 15 minutes of legal light.
- **Weight.** Many .350 Legend rifles (Ruger American, Savage Axis) are already lightweight budget guns. A scope that's needlessly heavy throws off the balance of the whole rifle.
- **Price relative to the rifle.** A common rule of thumb: don't spend less than a third of your rifle's cost on glass. On a $500 rifle, that puts you in the $150–250 range — minimum.

## BDC Reticle 101

A BDC reticle isn't magic — it's a series of aiming points below the main crosshair, each corresponding to a specific yardage, calibrated to your cartridge's actual drop. Here's the basic idea:

1. **Zero at 100 yards** using the primary crosshair, same as any scope.
2. **Range your target** with a rangefinder — guessing yardage defeats the purpose of a BDC.
3. **Turn magnification to maximum (9x).** Because these are Second Focal Plane (SFP) scopes, the reticle stays the same size while the target scales. This means your holdover hashmarks are **only accurate at full power**.
4. **Use the corresponding holdover point** — most straight-wall BDC reticles mark 150, 200, 250, and sometimes 300 yards.
5. **Hold dead-on at that point** and take the shot — no dialing turrets mid-hunt required.

The catch: a BDC reticle is only as accurate as the match between its calibration and your actual load. If you switch to a much lighter or heavier bullet than the reticle was calibrated for, your holdovers will be off. Stick close to standard factory-weight loads (like the 150-grain .350 Legend loads most of these reticles are built around) for the best real-world accuracy.

## Mounting & Eye Relief Notes

A few practical notes specific to lightweight straight-wall bolt guns:

- **Consider a [one-piece Picatinny rail](https://amzn.to/4aHRaRJ).** Many budget .350 Legend rifles (like the Ruger American or Savage Axis) have compact receivers with limited mounting space. A continuous, one-piece rail gives you significantly more slot options to slide your rings forward or backward to achieve perfect eye relief.
- **Ring height matters for larger lenses.** If you pick a scope with a 50mm objective bell (like the Vortex), you will likely need **Medium or High rings** to clear the rifle barrel. Keep this in mind, as higher rings can slightly alter how naturally your cheek welds to the stock. See scope rings that come in a variety of heights [here](https://amzn.to/4wAjpKO). Double check to make sure your rings are compatible with your scope before purchasing.
- **.350 Legend recoil is mild, but don't skip the eye relief check anyway.** None of these three cartridges will scope-bite you the way a hard-kicking magnum can, but a scope mounted too far back is still an easy, avoidable mistake — set eye relief with the rifle shouldered in your actual hunting position, not just at the bench.
- **Torque your rings to spec.** Lightweight rifles transmit more felt vibration to the scope than heavier guns — a properly torqued mount matters more here, not less.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a BDC reticle for a .350 Legend, or will a regular duplex work?** A standard duplex will get you dead-on hits at your zero distance, but you'll be guessing on holdover past 100 yards. Given how quickly straight-wall bullets drop, a BDC reticle calibrated for your cartridge meaningfully improves your odds on any shot past 150 yards.

**Will these scopes also work on a .400 Legend or .450 Bushmaster rifle?** The Vortex and Hawke reticles are both calibrated across multiple straight-wall cartridges, including .450 Bushmaster (and the Hawke also covers .400 Legend directly). The Leupold's CDS dial is specific to .350 Legend ballistics and wouldn't carry over accurately to a different cartridge.

**Is a 40mm or 50mm objective lens better for hunting?** A 50mm objective (like the Vortex) gathers more light, which helps in low-light conditions, but adds some weight and requires slightly higher scope rings to clear the barrel. A 40mm objective (Leupold, Hawke) keeps the rifle's profile lower and more balanced, which is plenty for typical straight-wall hunting distances under 200 yards.

**Do I need to buy rings and a base separately?** Yes — none of these scopes include mounting hardware. Make sure your rings match your scope's tube diameter (all three here use a 1" tube) and are rated for your rifle's action length.
