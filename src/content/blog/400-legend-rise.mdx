A new cartridge gets a launch-week press release from one company, then either fades or spreads. Three years in, the **.400 Legend** has spread. What started as a single Winchester/Savage partnership in 2023 is now offered across bolt-actions, single-shots, and AR-15 platforms from close to a dozen manufacturers — plus a growing aftermarket of parts makers who never touch a complete rifle but keep adding to the ecosystem anyway.

If you're trying to figure out whether the .400 Legend is a passing trend or a cartridge that's here to stay, the manufacturer lineup tells the story better than any marketing copy.

For the regulatory side of things — which states actually require a straight-wall cartridge in the first place — see our [full state-by-state SWC regulations guide](/blog/straight-wall-cartridge-state-regulations) rather than us re-covering it here.

## Why it exists: the .350 Legend left a gap

To understand why the .400 Legend showed up in the first place, it helps to look at what came before it. The .350 Legend, launched in 2019, is what opened this market — cheap ammo, mild recoil, and legal in every SWC state, which made it an easy first straight-wall rifle for a lot of Midwest hunters who'd been shooting slugs or muzzleloaders.

But the .350 also picked up a real, recurring complaint in the field: inconsistent blood trails. A lot of that traces back to standard cup-and-core factory loads not always getting a full pass-through, especially past 100–150 yards where velocity — and with it, expansion and penetration — drops off. No exit wound means less blood on the ground, which means a harder track. Worth being straight about this: shot placement and bullet selection matter more than the case design itself, and plenty of hunters have taken clean, short-tracking deer with a .350. But the complaint was common enough that it shaped how the next cartridge got built.

Winchester introduced the .400 Legend in April 2023 with an explicit purpose: sit between the .350 Legend and the harder-hitting .450 Bushmaster. More energy and effective range than the .350, without the .450's stout recoil and muzzle blast. Savage Arms partnered with Winchester Ammunition to bring the cartridge to market fast, launching it in the 110 Apex Hunter XP, 110 Hog Hunter, and Axis II XP, with 11 more Savage models chambered by the end of the year. Mossberg, Ruger, and CMMG all announced .400 Legend chamberings the same year.

That's an unusually fast, unusually broad rollout for a brand-new cartridge — most take years to pick up more than one or two manufacturers.

One more thing worth saying plainly, since it's a theme on this site: a bigger cartridge doesn't fix a bad hit. If the .400 Legend interests you because you want a cleaner track, that starts with shot placement and bullet choice, not just extra case capacity.

## 2024–2025: it keeps going

Mossberg added the .400 Legend to its Patriot bolt-action line in 2024 — a budget-friendly workhorse rifle, which matters for a straight-wall cartridge audience that (rightly) cares more about range time than a rifle with a fancy name on it.

## 2026 SHOT Show: the lineup keeps growing

By January 2026, the .400 Legend showed up across several new product lines:

- **Franchi** chambered its Momentum Elite (22" barrel) and Momentum Carbine Elite (18" barrel) bolt-actions in .400 Legend
- **CVA** added it to its updated Scout XP single-shot line and Scout Takedown
- **Traditions Firearms** added it to the Outfitter G3 line
- **Thompson/Center** offers a .400 Legend barrel for its Encore interchangeable-barrel platform

That's a manufacturer list that now spans bolt-action, single-shot, and switch-barrel platforms — not just one company doubling down on its own cartridge.

## The AR-15 aftermarket: not full guns, but it counts

A cartridge's staying power shows up in the aftermarket just as much as the factory rifle lineup. Several AR-15 parts makers now offer .400 Legend barrels and complete uppers, letting hunters convert an existing AR-15 lower rather than buying a whole new rifle:

- **Faxon Firearms** — Duty Series .400 Legend barrels, USA-manufactured
- **Bear Creek Arsenal** — complete .400 Legend uppers in multiple configurations
- **Moriarti Armaments** — .400 Legend barrels and upper assemblies
- **E. Arthur Brown Company** — .400 Legend AR-15 barrels, some marketed as simple swaps using a 6.8 SPC bolt carrier group

None of these companies build a complete rifle. But a healthy conversion-parts market is a real signal — it means enough hunters already own an AR-15 platform and want to add .400 Legend to it, rather than the cartridge only existing because one company is pushing it.

## What this means for you

If you're weighing the .400 Legend against the .350 Legend or .450 Bushmaster for your next straight-wall rifle, the manufacturer spread matters for a practical reason: **parts availability and resale value**. A cartridge with one rifle from one company is a bigger gamble than one with bolt-actions, single-shots, and AR conversions from eight-plus manufacturers. Three years in, the .400 Legend has cleared that bar.

That said — and this bears repeating — more money on a rifle chambered in the trendiest cartridge doesn't replace range time. Whatever straight-wall cartridge you land on, put rounds downrange before the season, not just cash on the counter.
