The combine does more scouting work in one afternoon than you will all September. The day a field comes down, every deer bedding inside or along it has to recalculate their whole routine — and most of them recalculate onto your fence line.

## What actually changes

A standing cornfield is cover. A cut cornfield is a cafeteria with no walls. Deer that were bedding in the third or fourth row in from the edge lose that option overnight, and they don't go far — they shift to the next-thickest cover within a few hundred yards, usually a fence line, a drainage ditch, or a CRP strip.

That shift is predictable enough to hunt before the deer have settled into a new pattern, which is the part most guys miss. They wait two weeks for "things to calm down." The first 72 hours after a cut is often the best sit you'll get all month, because the deer haven't picked a new bedroom yet and they're moving on instinct, not habit.

## How to use it without burning the spot

- Check combine progress from the road, not from inside the field. You don't need to walk it to know it's down.
- Move fast — a stand hung the evening of the cut, hunted the next morning, beats a "perfect" setup hung a week later.
- Don't hunt the field itself yet. Hunt the transition cover the deer are pushed into.

This is the kind of thing a satellite map from last winter can't tell you. You have to be watching the calendar and the gravel road at the same time.
